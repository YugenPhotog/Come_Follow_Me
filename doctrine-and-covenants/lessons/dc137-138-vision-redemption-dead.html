The Doctrinal Dilemma

Biblical Premise #1:
God is no respecter of persons.
(Acts 10:34)

Biblical Premise #2:
Baptism is essential for salvation.
(John 3:5)

These two premises created centuries of unresolved theological tension.

Early Christians, medieval theologians, Reformers, and Puritans all struggled with this dilemma. It produced deep anxiety, particularly for converts in Joseph Smith’s day.

Historical Witnesses:
Augustine (Enchiridion, ch. 93):
“It is not possible for unbaptized infants to be admitted to the kingdom of heaven.”

Thomas Aquinas (Summa Theologiae III, Q. 68, a. 9):
“Children deprived of baptism are excluded from the kingdom of heaven.”

Jonathan Edwards (Original Sin, 1758):
“Infants that die are still in a state of sin and condemnation… for they have no interest in Christ, and so must perish.”

Cotton Mather (The Fall of Adam, 1690):
“Infants dying unbaptized are still under the guilt of Adam, and have no right unto the salvation purchased by Christ.”

The Doctrinal Dilemma Solved

D&C 137

January 21, 1836

Kirtland Temple

Joseph Smith sees his brother Alvin—who died in 1823 without baptism—in the celestial kingdom.

D&C 138

October 3, 1918

Salt Lake City

Joseph F. Smith—who lost his father at age 5 and buried 13 children—sees how the gospel is preached to the dead.

D&C 137 (1836):
- Context: Pre-dedication of Kirtland Temple. Joseph is concerned about Alvin (died Nov 1823).
- Establishes the possibility of salvation for the unbaptized.

D&C 138 (1918):
- Context: WWI ending, Spanish Flu pandemic (October 1918 was the deadliest month).
- JFS was arguably the most "death-acquainted" prophet.
- Establishes the mechanism (spirit world missionary work).

God Judges by the Desires of the Heart

Let's Read: D&C 137:7–9

What does it teach us about God's character that He judges by what we would have done, not just what we did do?

How should this change how we think about family members or friends who died without hearing the gospel?

Pondering Opens the Eyes of Understanding

Let's Read: D&C 138:1–6, 11

What's the difference between reading scripture and pondering it? What did Joseph F. Smith do that we might not always do?

When have you received unexpected insight while studying scripture? What were the circumstances?

The Gospel Is Preached on Both Sides of the Veil

Let's Read: D&C 138:29–34

Why do you think Christ organized the righteous to teach rather than going Himself to spirit prison?

What does this structure teach us about the Lord's economy of labor—here and in the spirit world?

D&C 138:29–34. Key point: Christ's visit was administrative—He organized the forces. Discussion hooks: 1. Delegation allows others to grow (agency). 2. The priesthood order continues across the veil. 3. Invite brief personal experiences regarding the "thin veil."

They Cannot Be Saved Without Us

Let's Read: D&C 138:57–59

Why do you think the Lord designed it so the living and dead need each other?

Why do you think ordinances are such an essential part of the Lord’s system of redemption? What does this reveal about why temple work matters eternally?

How does this revelation help answer the problem of unequal mortal opportunity? What does it reveal about the Lord’s justice, mercy, and commitment to honoring the agency of all His children?

Saviors on Mount Zion

Let's Read: Obadiah 1:21 and D&C 128:15

For we without them cannot be made perfect; neither can they without us be made perfect.
— Joseph Smith, D&C 128:18

We Cannot Be Made Perfect Without Them

What does it mean that we "cannot be made perfect" without our ancestors? How are we connected to them in ways that affect our own salvation?

If we are to be "saviors on Mount Zion," what does that imply about our responsibility? How is that both humbling and empowering?

Doctrinal Summary

These revelations answer questions that have troubled believers for centuries.

God's plan is not arbitrary or exclusive—it is comprehensive and merciful.

Every soul will have the opportunity to choose.

And we have the privilege of participating in their redemption.
