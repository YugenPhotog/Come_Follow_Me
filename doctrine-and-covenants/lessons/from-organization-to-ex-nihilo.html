The 2nd Century Shift to Creation Ex Nihilo

How Greek Philosophy Changed Christian Theology

Understanding a Pivotal Theological Development

What We'll Explore

This presentation examines one of the most consequential theological shifts in Christian history:

  1. What early Christians believed about Creation
  2. How Greek philosophy influenced 2nd-century theology
  3. Why creation ex nihilo became dominant
  4. The theological consequences of this shift
  5. How this relates to LDS restored doctrine

Early Christian View

1st - Early 2nd Century

Christians inherited Jewish cosmology from Genesis:

  • Genesis 1:2 describes God organizing pre-existing matter: "the earth was without form, and void"
  • Early Christians taught God was a divine craftsman who organized chaos into cosmos
  • This paralleled Plato's Timaeus where the Demiurge shapes pre-existing matter
  • God was seen as organizer, not creator from absolute nothing

The Shift Begins

Mid-2nd Century Onward

The Context:

  • Church theologians encountered Greek philosophical schools (Platonism, Stoicism, Gnosticism)
  • They needed to distinguish Christianity from competing philosophies
  • Creation ex nihilo emerged as a way to emphasize God's absolute transcendence
  • Goal: Show God is utterly unique and different from all creation

Philosophical Influence #1

Middle Platonism

Greek Philosophical Beliefs:

  • An eternal, unchanging ultimate reality exists
  • Matter was seen as inherently inferior to spirit
  • To elevate God above all competitors, God must be utterly different from creation

The Solution:

God didn't work with existing materials—He created from absolute nothingness. This made God completely transcendent and unique.

Philosophical Influence #2

Anti-Gnostic Polemic

The Gnostic Problem:

  • Gnostics taught that an inferior deity (Demiurge) created the material world from flawed matter
  • They viewed matter as inherently evil
  • This contradicted Genesis 1:31: "God saw everything... and behold, it was very good"

Orthodox Christian Response:

To affirm that creation is good and that the same God who is Father is also Creator, they argued creation ex nihilo: God alone created everything, and it was inherently good.

Philosophical Influence #3

Defending Divine Sovereignty

The Concern:

  • If God worked with pre-existing matter, it suggested limitations on God's power
  • Philosophers asked: "If matter existed independently, isn't it co-eternal with God?"
  • This seemed to compromise God's uniqueness and supremacy

The Solution:

Ex nihilo doctrine made God the sole ultimate reality. Nothing exists independently of God's creative act. This preserved divine sovereignty and uniqueness.

Key Figures in the Shift

Theophilus of Antioch
Late 2nd Century
First Christian writer to explicitly use the phrase ex nihilo. Argued that God's power is shown in creating from nothing, not just shaping existing materials.
Irenaeus of Lyon
ca. 180 AD
Wrote against Gnostic heresies. Emphasized God alone is Creator, not a lesser being working with flawed matter.
Tertullian
ca. 200 AD
Developed the doctrine further in Latin theology. Made ex nihilo normative in Western Christianity.

Theological Consequences

1. Nature of God

Before

God as master craftsman, organizer, Father

After

God as absolutely transcendent, wholly other, infinite

Theological Consequences

2. Nature of Humans

Before

Humans as God's literal offspring with divine potential

After

Humans as God's creatures, fundamentally different in kind

The gap between Creator and creature became ontological (being itself), not just developmental

Theological Consequences

3. Possibility of Deification

Before

Humans could become like God (partakers of divine nature - 2 Peter 1:4)

After

Humans could be saved by God but never become like God

Deification persisted in Eastern Orthodoxy but faded in the West

Theological Consequences

4. Eternity of the Soul

  • If everything was created from nothing, souls were created too
  • This undermined the doctrine of pre-existence (that we existed as spirits before birth)
  • Made eternal progression conceptually impossible
  • Humans have a beginning in time, not eternal existence

The Irony

The 2nd-century shift was meant to elevate God's power and uniqueness, but it had unintended consequences:

  • Made God seem distant and alien rather than a loving Father
  • Replaced the intimate parent-child relationship with a master-servant dynamic
  • Lost the early Christian doctrine that we can become "partakers of the divine nature"

What seemed like a win for divine transcendence became a loss for divine relationship and human potential.

Joseph Smith's Restoration

Challenging Ex Nihilo

Joseph Smith's revelations directly challenged the doctrine of creation ex nihilo and recovered ancient truths that had been lost.

D&C 93:29

"Man was also in the beginning with God. Intelligence, or the light of truth, was not created or made, neither indeed can be."

Key Teaching: Human intelligence is eternal and was not created from nothing. We have always existed in some form.

Abraham 3:22-23

"Now the Lord had shown unto me, Abraham, the intelligences that were organized before the world was; and among all these there were many of the noble and great ones; And God saw these souls that they were good, and he stood in the midst of them, and he said: These I will make my rulers"

Key Teaching: Intelligences existed before creation and were organized, not created from nothing.

Joseph Smith's Teaching

"God never had the power to create the spirit of man at all. God himself could not create himself... Intelligence is eternal and exists upon a self-existent principle."

—King Follett Discourse, April 1844

What the Restoration Recovered

  • God as organizer, not creator from nothing
  • Eternal nature of intelligence/spirit
  • Humans as God's literal offspring
  • Potential for eternal progression
  • Deification as actual destiny, not metaphor
  • God as Father in the most literal sense
  • The doctrine of pre-existence

Conclusion

Understanding this historical shift helps us appreciate:

  1. Why most Christians today don't believe in deification
  2. How well-intentioned theological developments can obscure truth
  3. The profound significance of Joseph Smith's revelations
  4. Why the Restoration literally restored ancient Christian doctrines

The 2nd-century shift from organization to creation ex nihilo fundamentally changed Christianity's understanding of God, humanity, and our divine potential.
