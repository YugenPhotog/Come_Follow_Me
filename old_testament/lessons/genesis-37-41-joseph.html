This Question Has a Name

Theodicy (thee-OD-ih-see)

Greek: theos (God) + dike (justice)

If God is good and all-powerful — why does He allow His people to suffer?

  • Job — righteous man, stripped of everything, given no explanation
  • Jeremiah — faithful prophet, thrown into a pit
  • Joseph Smith — Liberty Jail, 1839: "O God, where art thou?" (D&C 121:1–2)
  • Joseph of Egypt — and now, this man

We are not going to resolve this in the abstract today. We are going to watch God answer it through one man's life.

Why God Allows Suffering

1. Free Will

Agency requires the possibility of opposition. God will not override human choices — suffering is often the consequence of a world where agency is real. (2 Nephi 2:11, 27)

2. Soul-Making

Suffering is the training ground for virtue and spiritual maturity. Earth life is designed to produce growth. (Abraham 3:25; D&C 122:7)

3. Greater Good

Some suffering leads to goods that could not exist otherwise — testimony, communal strength, canonized revelation from Liberty Jail. (Ether 12:6)

4. Mysterious Providence

Some suffering cannot be fully explained. God responds not always with answers, but with assurance. "My son, peace be unto thy soul." (D&C 121:7)

5. The LDS Distinctive — A Weeping God

God is not distant from our suffering. He entered it in Gethsemane. He weeps with us (Moses 7:28). Our suffering is consecrated — not just educational, but preparation for exaltation itself. Christ's empathy is not theoretical. It is earned. (Alma 7:11–12)

Suffering With Christ

The Deepest Answer — The Pauline Doctrine

Our suffering is not random, not pointless, not merely educational. When we suffer faithfully, we suffer with Christ — participating in something larger than ourselves.

Romans 8:17

Joint-heirs with Christ — if so be that we suffer with him. Suffering with Christ is the condition of joint-heirship.

Philippians 3:10

"The fellowship of his sufferings." Suffering with Christ is a form of knowing Him — intimate, participatory knowledge.

Colossians 1:24

Paul's sufferings fill up the afflictions of Christ in His body. Our suffering has redemptive meaning because it is joined to His.

Joseph didn't just endure suffering. He suffered redemptively — his pit, his prison, his false accusation all became instruments of salvation for his family and for Egypt. His suffering participated in something far larger than he could see.

So What Is the Answer to Theodicy?

God does not always prevent the pit. But He enters it with us.

His presence gives us the power to bear what we cannot bear alone — and through that bearing, we become something we could not have become any other way.

And when we suffer faithfully, we do not suffer alone — we suffer with Christ, participating in something redemptive, something larger than we can see.
