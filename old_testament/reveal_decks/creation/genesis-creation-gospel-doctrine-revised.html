"In the Beginning
God Created the Heaven
and the Earth"

Genesis 1–2 · Moses 2–3 · Abraham 4–5

January 12–18, 2026

Welcome to our lesson on the Creation. This week we study three accounts: Genesis, Moses, and Abraham—each offering unique insights into why God created the earth and what it means for us.

Three Witnesses of Creation

  • Genesis — The biblical account, preserved through Moses
  • Moses 2–3 — Restored vision given to Moses (June 1830)
  • Abraham 4–5 — Abraham's vision of the pre-mortal council

Each witness adds unique insights to help us understand why we were created.

Point out that having three accounts follows the scriptural pattern of establishing truth through multiple witnesses. Moses received his vision shortly after the Church was organized. Abraham's account gives us the pre-mortal perspective—the Gods counseling together.

1. Jesus Christ Created the Earth

Let's Read: Moses 2:1

"By mine Only Begotten I created these things"

The Creator and the Redeemer are the same person. He who made the mountains also descended below all things.

Read Moses 2:1 together. Emphasize: This is a profound truth unique to Restoration scripture. The Creator and the Redeemer are the same person. He who separated light from darkness also descended into spiritual darkness in Gethsemane. This connects Creation to Atonement in a deeply personal way.

Pause and Reflect

What does it mean to you that Christ is both your Creator and your Redeemer?

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

Give students 60 seconds of silent reflection to write in their study guides. Then invite 2-3 people to share briefly (one sentence each). This prevents monopolization and ensures more voices are heard.

2. God Creates Order from Chaos

Let's Read: Genesis 1:2 and Abraham 4:2

The earth was "without form, and void" ... "empty and desolate"

Yet God transformed it into something magnificent.

The Hebrew tohu va-vohu (formless and void) describes primordial chaos. God organized existing matter into beauty.

Read both scriptures together. Explain: The Hebrew "tohu va-vohu" describes primordial chaos. God didn't create from nothing but organized existing matter. Similarly, He doesn't discard us when we're chaotic—He works with what exists to create something beautiful.

Pause and Reflect

When has your life felt chaotic? How did (or could) the Lord bring order?

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

60 seconds of silent writing. Then invite 2-3 brief shares. Be ready to share your own example if needed to get discussion started.

3. Created in God's Image

Let's Read: Genesis 1:26–27

"So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him"

We are literally created in God's image. This gives every person inherent worth and divine potential.

Read Genesis 1:26-27 together. This is one of the most distinctive LDS doctrines. We believe God has a glorified, physical body and that we are literally created in His image—not merely metaphorically. This doctrine (Imago Dei) gives every human being inherent worth and divine potential.

Pause and Reflect

How does knowing you're created in God's image affect how you see yourself and others?

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

Silent reflection time. Ask: How should this affect how we treat others? How we see ourselves when we struggle? Invite responses from people who haven't shared yet.

4. Marriage: Equal Partners

Let's Read: Genesis 2:18

"I will make him an help meet [ezer kenegdo]"

Ezer = "strong helper" (used for God helping His people)

Kenegdo = "equal partner, corresponding to him"

"Fathers and mothers are obligated to help one another as equal partners." — Family Proclamation

The phrase "help meet" has been tragically misunderstood. Eve is not Adam's subordinate—she's his powerful ally who brings essential capacities he lacks. Ezer is used most often for GOD helping His people (Psalm 121). God said it was "not good" for Adam to be alone—Eve isn't a nice addition, she's ESSENTIAL.

Pause and Reflect

How does ezer kenegdo change your understanding of marriage?

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

Silent reflection. Then ask: What difference does it make to understand Eve as Adam's "strong helper who is his equal" rather than subordinate? How does this challenge cultural assumptions?

5. Stewards of God's Creations

Let's Read: Genesis 1:28

"Replenish the earth, and subdue it: and have dominion"

The Hebrew radah implies caring governance—like a shepherd, not a tyrant. We are stewards, not owners.

D&C 121: dominion by "persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, meekness, and love"

Elder Gérald Caussé spoke on "Our Earthly Stewardship" (Oct 2022). Radah means to rule, but in context of God's character, it implies caring governance. This applies to the environment, animals, and all of God's creations.

Pause and Reflect

What does righteous "dominion" look like vs. exploitation? Give one example.

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

Silent writing time. Look for concrete examples: how we treat animals, care for the environment, use natural resources, etc.

6. God Blessed the Sabbath Day

Let's Read: Genesis 2:2–3

"God ... rested on the seventh day ... and sanctified it"

God didn't rest because He was tired—He rested to establish a pattern for us. The Sabbath is a gift, not a burden.

President Nelson invites us to make it a "delight"

The Hebrew "shabbat" means "to cease" or "to rest." The Sabbath was "made for man" (Mark 2:27). Ask: What specific practices make it delightful for you?

Pause and Reflect

What makes the Sabbath "blessed" or delightful to you personally?

⏱ Take 60 seconds to write down your answer before we discuss.

Silent reflection. Invite people to share specific practices that make the Sabbath meaningful. This is very personal—honor different approaches.
